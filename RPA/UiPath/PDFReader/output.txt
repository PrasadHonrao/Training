PREFACE
With years of practice and usage, we all are well familiar with Centralized Version Control Systems
(CVCS) like SVN, VSS, and T FS etc., however many of us are still not well knowledgeable about
Distributed Version Control Systems (DVCS) like Git and its inherent advantages. With the
dominance of GitHub which hosts many well-known open source projects like Angular, jQuery,
node etc., it is even more important to know Git API and different ways to interact with hosts like
GitHub, Visual Studio Online and T FS etc.
The purpose of this document is to introduce you to both CVCS, DVCS with their advantages and
limitations. Once we have the basic understanding of it, we will do a deep dive into commonly
used GIT commands with their syntax. 4Git's Most Wanted

Overview
Centralized version control systems are based on the idea that there is a single central copy of
your project somewhere (probably on a server), and programmers will commit or check-in their
changes to this central copy. Committing a change simply means recording the change in the
central system. After the commit, other programmers can then see this change. They can also pull
down the change, and the version control tool will automatically update the contents of any files
that were changed in the local repository. In such case, programmers no longer have to keep
many copies of files on their hard drives manually, because the version control tool can talk to the
central copy and retrieve any version they need on the fly. Some of the most common centralized
version control systems you may have heard of or used are CVS, Subversion (or SVN) and Visual
Source Safe (VSS).
When you're working with a centralized version control system, your workflow for adding a new
feature or fixing a bug in your project will usually look something like this:
o Pull down any changes other people have made from the central server.
Make your changes, and make sure they work properly.
Commit your changes to the central server, so other programmers can see them.
